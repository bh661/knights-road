Afterword — on what this game argues

Two voices have been running beside each other for the last twenty minutes. Neither was permitted to call the other wrong. That is the one argument this game is making: that Cervantes, in Part I, declines to tell his reader which voice to trust, and that the refusal is the novel's most serious and most generous act. The man in the cage has done something with his middle years that most of the men who read about him never do. Whether you call it madness, folly, a delay against death, or a life — Cervantes will not say, and this game will not say.

How to read

The screen is divided into two columns. The left column is Don Quijote's first-person voice — his account of what is happening and what it means. The right column is the outer record: the narrator, bystanders, and the reported speech of other characters.

Both columns describe the same event. They never agree on what the event is. Neither is permitted to call the other correct.

You are not being asked to decide who is right. You are being asked to sit inside the tension.
